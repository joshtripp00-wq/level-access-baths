How Much Does an Accessible Shower Cost in Omaha?

9 min read · Level Access Baths Learning Center

If you've started researching accessible showers, you've probably noticed something frustrating: almost nobody publishes prices. You get "request a quote" buttons, high-pressure in-home sales visits, and wildly different numbers from different companies. This guide is our attempt to fix that for Omaha and Council Bluffs homeowners — real ranges, what moves the number, and how to compare quotes intelligently.

"All-in" is the key phrase. Our numbers include demolition, haul-away, full waterproofing, materials, fixtures, installation, and cleanup. Many advertised prices don't — which is how a "$4,999 special" becomes a $9,000 invoice.

The Five Factors That Actually Drive Your Price

1. What's coming out

A fiberglass tub pops out in pieces. A cast iron tub has to be cut apart and carried out in sections — it's heavy, slow, and exactly the kind of thing that generates "surprise" charges from contractors who didn't ask the right questions up front. (We ask. It's priced in from day one.)

2. The entry style you choose

A low-threshold base is the standard conversion. True zero-entry — where the bathroom floor flows into the shower with no lip at all — requires engineering the slope into the floor structure, which is more work and more skill. If a wheelchair or rolling walker is part of the picture, zero-entry is worth every dollar. If not, low-threshold usually serves perfectly. More on that decision in our zero-entry vs. low-threshold guide.

3. Wall material

This is the biggest quality fork in the road. Solid-surface panels are seamless, grout-free, and essentially maintenance-free for decades. Cheap acrylic liner kits glued over existing surfaces cost less on day one and routinely fail within years. Tile looks beautiful but reintroduces grout — the thing most of our clients are happiest to never scrub again.

4. Options and upgrades

Built-in or fold-down seating, glass enclosures instead of a curtain, recessed storage niches, upgraded fixture finishes, rain heads. Each adds modestly to the price; none should ever be sprung on you mid-project.

5. Who's doing the work

National franchise brands fund television advertising and commissioned sales forces — and that overhead lives inside their quotes. Local specialists with their own installation teams typically deliver the same or better materials with more of your dollar going into the actual work.

How to Compare Quotes Like a Pro

  • Demand all-in scope. Does the number include demo, haul-away, waterproofing, fixtures, and cleanup? Get it in writing.
  • Ask about the waterproofing system. "We caulk it really well" is the wrong answer. There should be a membrane system behind the walls.
  • Ask who actually installs. Employees who do this daily, or whichever subcontractor was available?
  • Ask about the warranty — specifically leaks. Ours is lifetime on the installation. Shorter warranties tell you what the installer expects.
  • Ask what happens if they find damage behind the wall. The answer reveals whether the quote was honest.

Funding worth checking: VA HISA/SAH/SHA grants for qualifying veterans, Nebraska and Iowa Medicaid HCBS waivers, and some long-term-care insurance policies all sometimes fund bathroom accessibility work. We're familiar with the paperwork and can help point you the right way.

The Bottom Line

For most Omaha homeowners, a professionally installed accessible shower is a $6,000-and-up project — comparable to a few months of assisted living costs, except you only pay it once and you stay home. The number on your quote matters less than what's inside it: insist on all-in pricing, real waterproofing, reinforced grab bar blocking, and a warranty the installer expects to honor.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the cheapest way to make a shower accessible?

A professionally installed grab bar package is the lowest-cost meaningful upgrade — ours runs $650 with bars anchored into reinforced blocking, not drywall anchors. It doesn't remove the tub barrier, but it dramatically reduces fall risk while you plan a larger project.

Why do quotes from different contractors vary so much?

Three reasons: scope (is demo and haul-away included? waterproofing? cleanup?), materials (solid-surface panels vs. cheap acrylic glue-up kits), and business model (national franchises carry heavy advertising and commission costs that show up in your price). Always compare what's included, not just the bottom-line number.

Are there hidden costs I should watch for?

With some contractors, yes — 'discovered' subfloor damage, disposal fees, and change orders are classic mid-project additions. Ask up front: is the quote all-in? Does it cover demolition, haul-away, and cleanup? What happens if you find rot behind the wall? Our answer: one number, and we stand by it.

Does insurance, Medicare, or the VA help pay?

Original Medicare generally doesn't cover home modifications. But qualifying veterans can use VA HISA, SAH, or SHA grants; Nebraska and Iowa Medicaid HCBS waivers sometimes fund modifications; and some long-term-care insurance policies contribute. Worth checking before you assume it's all out of pocket.

Is a cheap acrylic liner kit a real alternative?

Big-box liner kits glued over your existing tub or surround cost less up front but trap moisture, fail at the seams, and don't address the actual accessibility barrier. If budget is the constraint, a phased approach — grab bars now, conversion later — beats a liner that has to be torn out anyway.
